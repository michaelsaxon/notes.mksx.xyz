“Our reviewing system is broken” is a huge cliche. Ever more papers are being submitted to AI, ML, NLP, and CV conferences and there’s no end in sight. As the submission counts balloon, conferences are forced to adopt increasingly desperate measures, including drafting potentially unqualified reviewers from author lists, and these reviewers submit increasingly lazy and low-quality reviews. In the face of the low-quality review crisis, conferences look for new ways to poke, prod, and punish authors into writing better reviews.

Complaints about review quality on social media are perennial. At ICLR, one conference that has a radically open review process with publicly visible submissions, comments, and reviews, a different drama involving some outrageous review comes up every year. Authors and reviewers alike are exhausted. These conferences are in a death spiral---the current state of affairs is unsustainable.

I certainly am exhausted. What can we do to fix it?

Here I will meditate on the purpose of peer review and what it has become. I will discuss how our publication practices interact with institutional incentives to effectively favors a Zerg rush strategy1, where authors can win by submitting as many papers as fast as they can.

I will argue that rather than continuing to conscript and constrain reviewers further, or introducing bizarre new constraints on submissions based on arbitrary external factors, we need to address this problem at its root and make it harder to publish for everyone. We must stop the flood.

How we got here

TIP

tl;dr: the purposes of peer review are (1) deciding what gets published, and (2) critiquing and improving work. The purpose of publishing is classically to establish precedence, disseminate work, and produce an archival record, but modern conferences are no longer necessary channels for any of those things. There are two pragmatic purposes to publishing in CS conferences: accumulating CV line items for career advancement and justifying the travel itself. This leaves only the critique value of peer review, provided in the acceptance signal, to be filled by conferences. Are conference papers the right way to provide this signal?

What is the purpose of peer review?

The primary purpose of peer review is to adjudicate whether a contribution is novel and valid for the purposes of credit attribution, disseminating new work and producing an archival record of research.

Peer review was effectively invented for these purposes by Henry Oldenburg of the Royal Society of London with his journal Philosophical Transactions. From the British Parliament website:

The first authors of a phenomenon or result wanted their priority as discoverer to be publicly acknowledged and secured before they were prepared to share their results with their colleagues. Oldenburg realised [sic] that an independent periodical publication run by an independent third-party that would faithfully record the name of a discoverer, the date the paper was submitted and a description of the discovery could resolve this dilemma for the pioneering scientists of his age.

Oldenburg would personally adjudicate which submissions would or wouldn’t be included in the transactions, and in doing so created the first documented instance of peer review. From the same site:

The four functions of Oldenburg’s journal: registration, dissemination, peer review and archival record are so fundamental to the way scientists behave and how science is carried out that all subsequent journals, even those published electronically in the 21st century, have conformed to Oldenburg’s model.

In addition to providing a dissemination signal, peer review also ideally serves to strengthen the quality of work by soliciting critique from a fresh set of eyes.

The question of what peer review is for in a research community thus collapses to this question: what is the purpose of publishing?

Why we publish (why we should?)

At its most ideal, the purpose of publishing pretty much circles back to the same functions intended by Oldenburg, to establish precedence of, disseminate, and archive research. Which of these functions are currently satisfied?

We live in an era of arXiv and Twitter. arXiv and its ilk have already supplanted the precedence establishment function of publication, as preprints are timestamped upon upload.

The gatekeeping function of publishing is irrelevant now that manuscripts are out in public, often before they are even submitted, and are publicly searchable and publicly disseminated outside of many official channels. In our field, things are moving so fast that venues can’t keep up. In the time between a preprint’s submission to a conference and its appearance in the proceedings, several follow up works may already be out.

Finally, the curation function of publishing is obsolete too, as conferences now publish way too many papers. If you read one paper a day, it would take 16 years to get through all the papers in NeurIPS 2025 alone. Even though there are orders of magnitude more papers on the arXiv, it feels like a distinction without a subjective difference, like comparing the size of N to R. I will never even glance through all the titles of either.

Many in the community (myself included) do not take the peer reviewed-ness of a paper into consideration when deciding to read it. And why should we, in light of the above?

I think one can fairly argue that current CS publication venues do not fulfill any of Oldenburg’s original purposes. Precedence and archiving are both fulfilled by preprint servers. Dissemination is handled by social media, and the dissemination function of archival conferences is hampered by the overwhelming length and lack of coherence in their proceedings.

The intellectual contributions in the form of research itself sits at the center of these purposes. Pragmatically, the purpose of publishing in ML is to generate new CV beans2 and justify conference attendance3.

CV beans are useful and important! Everyone needs jobs, university admittance, and career advancement, and the bean counters in academia like to see more papers. I see the fib that “too many papers is a negative signal” bandied about from time to time. While I agree that ideally this would be the case, the fact is that according to the vast majority of bean counters, paper quantity is a neutral-to-positive signal. The real signal the counters care about is impact, as measured in citations and general buzz.

Why we conference publish

When I called conference attendance “grant-funded international vacations” above I was perhaps being too flippant. There are obviously real benefits to conference attendance beyond the travel itself, otherwise we would just be going on vacations instead of attending conferences.

Conferences offer a vital chance to catch up with your peers, learn what’s new, and grow your network---something that’s especially critical for the less-networked researchers who benefit less from the discoverability afforded papers on social media.

The consequences

It’s too easy to publish. Because of the high variance of reviews, and the small size of a minimum publishable unit, it’s very feasible to turn around a top conference paper inside of three months. In the real sciences, it is normal for a PhD student to publish perhaps one first author paper throughout their PhD. In other engineering fields, the traditional “three papers to graduate” bar is typically met by a student with exactly three papers. Only in CS do we have a tradition of “three best papers (out of many) to graduate.” It is normal for a productive PhD student to get 4-10 first-author papers out over the course of their PhD! That’s to say nothing about high school and undergrad papers.

Most of the elite top performers achieve this impact through honest dedication to their intuition, and then luck out when the ideas they intuited grow popular. However, the luck element here is key. Because there is luck involved, another way to get a few hits out is to pull as many lottery tickets as you can, in the form of as many low-hanging fruit submissions as possible across a broad array of topics. Because paper count is either ignored or rewarded, this strategy only has upside. You can even cite yourself in this deluge to make all your other papers that much more impactful. Join the Zerg!

Philosophy

First, I want to lay down the guiding philosophy in my proposal. If you disagree with the specific suggestions, you probably also disagree with me on one of these points.

Process principles

  1. We should be optimizing for sustainability. The longer we wait to fix this, the worse things are going to get, and the biggest unsustainability factor is the number of submissions.
  2. Perfect is probably impossible. The current system is unfair and must change. We should not be afraid of new sorts of unfairness in the pipeline, especially in the service of sustainability.
  3. Some gatekeeping is acceptable. A reviewer’s time is a privilege, not an entitlement. Some level of community buy-in and reviewer respect (in the form of submission quality). We are under no obligation as reviewers to give our time to anyone who asks, or to look at low-effort work.
    1. “I need to get papers for XYZ” is not a valid goal to structure the community around. There is going to be an endless procession of people who “need” an endless amount of help to achieve their goals. Do you owe everyone who asks your time and mentorship? Do you owe everyone who asks admittance into your lab, or to hire them? Why do we accept this logic around
  4. Paper decisions based on paper factors. We need to stop trying to socially engineer reviewing or authorship behavior with paper decisions. This flawed logic is what brought us the problematic (and now repealed) ACL anonymity embargo policy. This principle is in tension with (3), as reviewing is a form of community buy-in.
  5. Forced service work is bad service work. When we force people to review against their will when it isn’t convenient for them, we shouldn’t be surprised when they give out bad reviews.
  6. The purpose of publishing should not be handing out CV beans. We are under no obligation to structure venues in the service of facilitating easy career advancement. If we do it right, we can lower the tempo of publishing for everyone, and no individual is disadvantaged.

Current status

The ideal purpose of peer review is ignored as authors prioritize “getting papers in” over validating the ideas within. The Goodhart’s Law-ification of academic publishing is a well-worn concept at this point, and I don’t think it’s realistic to expect a conference policy change to magically make authors idealistic and scientific.

Proposal

Given this tension between the idealized and pragmatic purposes of publication, some have proposed more radical reworkings of CS publication practices. Yann LeCun’s 2009 proposed publishing model effectively suggested doing away with set venues entirely and attaching public reviews to papers hosted in a public repository. This proposal eventually influenced OpenReview, which is effectively only distinct from prior reviewing platforms in quality of life features and enabling the public reviewing process used at ICLR.

Community-level rather than conference-level review ratio

I am now going to tell a little tangent about internet piracy. I promise it’s relevant.

The file sharing (torrenting) community actually suffers from a very similar free rider problem to conference reviewing. When you download a file with BitTorrent you connect to many seeders who each have copies of the same file, and get sent different chunks from different seeders until the file is downloaded. A user’s ability to leech (download) is entirely dependent on other users’ choices to seed.

Public trackers (sites which maintain links to torrents, e.g. the Pirate Bay) allow anyone who has the link to a torrent to download it on the generosity of a relatively small number of prolific seeders4.

Consequently, a few problems arise with these trackers. Most users leech without seeding, a so-called hit-and-run, getting a free ride to download a file using someone else’s bandwidth. Given this costly and asymmetrical relationship, many seeders give up on hosting most files over time. For the effective main use case of these trackers, downloading new movies and TV episodes, this works fine. New files get uploaded, burn brightly, and go away. The torrent links to obscure files die out, rendering them inaccessible5.

Private trackers are an approach to dealing with these users. Torrent links are only available to invited users, and their leeching and seeding are tracked. They do this to track a user’s upload/download ratio, and require that users maintain a positive ratio to remain in good standing, or else be banned from the tracker. Crucially, Consequently, private trackers are much more sustainable.
