Revised Structure

Intro

  • This is what highly successful networkers do
    • I’m documenting the practices and mindset of people who excel at building genuine friendships in research communities
  • I’m an extrovert, but this is learnable
    • This comes naturally to me, but the skills and mindset shifts are teachable
  • Who this is for
    • Junior researchers, grad students, postdocs starting out in the community
    • People who feel awkward at conferences or unsure how to build connections
  • What we’re NOT talking about
    • This is not a “networking for jobs” guide (though networking helps with that)
      • Folksy wisdom from Michael’s family: better lucky than good
    • This is not about being fake or transactional
    • This is not about collecting business cards or maximizing your reach
  • The underlying thesis
    • Being good at this is a learnable skill, separate from how good your research is—both matter for a fulfilling career

I. Philosophy & Mindset

  • Why this matters
    • Your research friends are your longest friends (unlike non-research friends who diverge)
      • e.g., mom’s advice: “your sister will be your longest friend”—research friends are similar
      • First of many Folksy wisdom from Michael’s family
    • It’s fun and very fulfilling to invest in these friendships early—you’ll want to help each other
    • Long-term everyone moves together as peers
  • The right approach
    • Make friends, not “connections” or networking
    • Authentic relationships > transactional vibes
    • Your goal should be to have fun and make friends
    • Don’t enter conversations with an agenda (people can smell it)
      • e.g., people coming up with “I did this work, can you intro me to William?”
    • Enter with goal of having good conversation and getting to know people
    • Adopt the mindset: everyone here is your peer (not a teacher/student relationship)
      • This applies everywhere: with seniors at conferences, in online interactions, in long-term relationships
  • Who are you as a scientist?
    • Think holistically about who you are and what your career is beyond first-author papers
    • Value comes in many forms: open-source code, blogposts, surveys, service work, advocacy, good criticism
    • All of these build your name in the research community
    • What are you an expert in? What questions are you looking to answer?
      • Being known for depth in a specific area is more valuable than being known for everything

II. Building Your Profile

  • Why it matters
    • Having a visible profile means people recognize you at conferences and think positively of you before you even meet
      • e.g., people have recognized my badge and come up to recommend me, even without knowing exactly why
  • Be present and authentic
    • Share across many modalities: shitposts, memes, genuine opinions, grad life, complaining about reviews, hobbies, current events
    • SIGBOVIK papers and jokes
      • e.g., an entire joke paper that got a lot of attention
    • Advocate for things you authentically care about
      • e.g., advocating against ACL anonymity
    • The wedge: visibility compounds even if you post cringe—low effort, authentic posts matter
    • You can gain followers through good work, but also through making people care
  • Build concrete value through curation and expertise
    • Post about papers in your area (share links, discuss, give takeaways)
      • Establishes you as an expert in that area and helps others surface good work
      • e.g., be like the Aran Komatsuzaki of your specific area—more specific and insightful commentary delivers more value
    • Open-source code and repositories
    • Blogposts, tutorials, surveys
    • Service work and good criticism
    • Have a website

III. Making Friends (Conversation Skills)

  • How to talk to people
    • Ask about their work, not yours (people like talking about their work)
    • Start with sentence-or-two summary, let them guide with questions
    • If you’re hoping to work with someone: “I like your work, I’ve been working on related stuff, I’d like to collaborate” then wait
      • e.g., Matt Gardner’s framing: it’s low-key, flattering, easy to respond to
    • Don’t torpedo the conversation (read the room, don’t push annoying debates)
      • e.g., conversational kamikaze: one bad interaction gets wedged easily—don’t be afraid of momentary awkwardness
    • Don’t enter conversations with an agenda (people can smell it)
      • e.g., people coming up with “I did this work, can you intro me to William?”
  • Finding connection points
    • Discover mutual friends or connections (shared co-authors, advisors, etc.)
    • Find personalized hooks: background, interests, off-topic conversations you already know about them
      • e.g., “I like your tweets” or asking Chris Manning about Australian deadly wildlife/huntsman spiders
    • Have off-topic conversations with everyone, not just same-level peers (especially seniors)
      • Deep, genuine off-topic conversations show you see them as full people, not just their research
    • Have non-clichéd conversation starters ready (not “what do you think of the conference?” but “what are you really sick of seeing here?“)
    • Having opinions about research and how it’s done matters—being funny in a research context helps
  • Ending the conversation well
    • Twitter/Bluesky as rolodex: when you meet people, ask for their handle and follow (they’ll likely follow back)
    • Follow up within 24 hours with specific references and resource links
    • Maintain via emails/visits/coffee
    • Mutual friends beget more friends

IV. Conference Strategy

  • Before you arrive
    • Pre-identify who you want to meet broadly: not just senior famous profs, but people whose work interests you
      • Email junior folks ahead to schedule meetings (easier than professors, and junior people especially appreciate being recognized)
      • why: everyone is validated by being appreciated, junior people aren’t receiving as much
    • Ask friends what papers they like and which authors are attending to expand your list
    • Have a default hang-out buddy or easy mode: tag along with a senior mentor (they introduce you, cover more ground together with different connections)
      • e.g., having a “default hang out buddy” makes everything less socially scary and you cover more ground with mutual friend introductions
    • Prepare elevator pitches
    • Have calendar ready
  • During the conference
    • Poster sessions: live-tweet and engage actively
      • When you see a poster you like, have a conversation, take a picture, post YOUR OWN TAKEAWAYS (not just restating the abstract)
      • Give your specific insights and 1 key takeaway you learned
      • @ the authors to build connection and add to your rolodex
      • e.g., set a target number of papers to post about per day
      • This builds your expertise log, connects you with peers, and serves the community
    • Hallway track > attending every talk (don’t feel guilty about skipping talks for good conversations)
    • Meals are anchor points—seek out lunch with people you don’t know (catered meals = everyone sitting)
    • Socials/parties fill up first day; tag team in small groups to scaffold friends-of-friends
    • Don’t leave the conference to hang out unless it’s with new people
    • Join existing conversation circles at coffee breaks (squeeze in, people make space)
    • Force yourself to speak English the whole conference (especially at posters)
    • When NOT to approach (obviously busy/in meetings/in-depth conversations)
    • Be the person who organizes plans (people appreciate this; they like doing things but not organizing them)
    • Once plans are fixed, they’re FIXED (don’t change/bail)

Final takeaways

3 closing points:

  1. It’s a long game - You’re not trying to maximize connections at one conference. You’re building relationships that will span your entire career. The people you meet this year will be your colleagues, collaborators, and friends for decades.
  2. It compounds - Small investments in visibility, authentic conversations, and genuine friendships build on each other. A tweet, a follow-up email, showing up to organize dinner—these seem small but they accumulate into real professional relationships and opportunities.
  3. It’s actually fun - The best part is that this isn’t some grinding networking obligation. Making friends in your research community, having interesting conversations, and building a reputation for good work should be enjoyable. If it’s not, you’re probably doing it wrong.

Homework/discussion topic for before NeurIPS:

Pick one thing to try and report back:

  • For your profile: Post 3 things on Twitter/Bluesky before the conference (doesn’t have to be polished—could be a reaction to a paper you read, a hot take, a meme, whatever feels authentic). Notice how it feels to put yourself out there.
  • For the conference: Identify 2-3 junior researchers whose work you find interesting and email them before NeurIPS suggesting a coffee chat or lunch. Try framing it genuinely (“I’ve been excited about your recent work on X”) rather than transactionally.
  • For conversations: Think about one person you want to meet at the conference and find one personalized hook about them (a paper you liked, something they tweeted, mutual friend, etc.). Practice how you’d bring it up naturally.
